Equivalence of two security notions for symmetric encryption

Symmetric encryption should provide confidentiality, i.e., leak no information about a message except for its length (since an adversary can infer the length of the message from the length of the ciphertext). So, an encryption of a message m should look like an encryption of a message consisting of |m| zeroes. This notion is captured by indistinguishability under chosen plaintext attacks (IND-CPA), where the adversary chooses a message m and submits it to an ENC oracle which either returns an encryption of m or an encryption of |m| zeroes. The adversary can query the oracle multiple times.

An equally valid way of defining confidentiality is that whatever knowledge the adversary gets from ciphertexts, the adversary should have been able to simulate this knowledge just given the length of the message |m|. This simulation-based definition is usually called semantic security of encryption and is equivalent to the aforementioned game-based IND-CPA definition of confidentiality.

See here for an SSP-style proof.

Note: Often, IND-CPA security is also defined as a left-or-right distinguishing game which allows the adversary to submit two messages m0 and m1 of equal length and the game either encrypts the left or the right message. This notion is (up to a factor 2) equivalent to the aforementioned real-or-zeroes formulation, because encryptions of m0 and m1 are both indistinguishable from encryptions of |m0|=|m1| zeroes and because we can choose one of the two messages to be the all-zeroes message.

See Chapter 7 of Rosulek's The Joy of Cryptography for constructions and more discussion of security definitions for symmetric encryption.
